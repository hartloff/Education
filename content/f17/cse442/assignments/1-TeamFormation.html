== Project and Team Formation
Requirement: Form or join a team.
Verify: You name will be listed on the project page for your team
Your choice of project will have a significant impact on your experience in this course.

You are free to choose any software project you desire for this course.

To help you find a project and team you can discuss ideas with other students in the course on Ryver We will also discuss project ideas during the first week of class.

Whatever you choose for your project and team you must fulfill the following criteria:

  • Teams must have 3-5 members
  • Once your team is formed, create you project on the course website and fill in all required information

If you prefer to be placed in a random group, send an email to hartloff@buffalo.edu with "CSE442: Random Group" as the subject line. So long as it is mathematically possible to place all such students into groups of size 3-5, all students making such requests will be placed into groups together.

Recommended: Form a team around a common interest or specific project idea. This course is much more enjoyable if the whole team is interested in the project.
Recommended: Use caution when forming a team with 3 members. If a member resigns the course your may need to disband and join other teams mid-semester. If you have 3 members, make sure all three are dedicated to completing the course.
== Ryver
Requirement: Create a public team forum and a private team channel.
Verify: The river icon will appear on your project page which links to your public channel

We will use Ryver as the primary means of communication in this course (see syllabus for sign up link). For your project you will need to create the following on Ryver:

  • A public forum and submit the channel name on the course web site under your project details. This forum will be the primary means for anyone outside the team who wants to you about your project. (Verify that the link displays on your project page and that it is accurate)
  • A private team on Ryver and invite Jesse into your team. This team will be used to privately discuss any grading decisions or other sensitive information with your group.

== Team Contract
Requirement: Sign and submit a team contract.
Verify: Physically hand the signed contract to Jesse. The course staff will verify that all the required content is in the contract and that it is signed by all team members

Deliver a document outlining your project and the roles of each team member.

The team contract must be printed and signed by all team members, then submitted to the course instructor by the end of week 2. This contract will be kept on file and will be used throughout the semester. You are bound to this contract and its contents will be used to determine grading decisions. Any violations of this contract by a team member may result in a grade reduction for that person. If your group wishes to change your contract you can submit a replacement as long as all team members agree to and sign the new contract. These changes must be made in-between project submissions (ie. You can't redefine your MVP the day before the MVP deadline).

Your team contract must containing the following sections and details:


Project Outline

Recommended: Look through the criteria for all 7 project submissions before finalizing your contract.

Provide a high-level outline of your project and the technologies involved. This should give a clear idea of your vision for the project and a general idea of how that vision will become a reality.

In addition to the ideas and technologies, this section must clearly define your Minimum Viable Product (MVP) and at least 3 potential add-on features that can be added to this MVP. By th end of the semester you will be required to complete and deploy your MVP and at least one of these add-on features. Since only one add-on features needs to be completed, do not hesitate to list difficult features. All the features combined should show the true potential that your project could become.

MVP: As the name implies, when choosing your MVP you should choose the minimal amount of functionality needed to deliver a viable product. Think about the absolute minimal feature set that can still be useful to the end user and choose that. Anything that isn't absolutely necessary should be used as an add-on feature.

Add-on Feature: These are additional features that your project can benefit from having. These features must enable the end user to accomplish something that they could not do without it (ie. stylistic or performance changes will not count as add-on features in this course. They must add functionality for the end user to your software).

Recommended: If your idea is very ambitious, don't put the whole project in your MVP. Identify a simple core in the project and make that your MVP with everything else as add-on features. You must complete and release your MVP and at least 1 add-on feature this semester so don't commit to more work than your team can handle. Work on more add-on features to complete the full vision if you have time.

Team Roles

With the project defined you will now divide the work of the project among you team members. It is highly recommended that you take your time on this section and ensure that everyone is satisfied with their roles. If this section is lacking it may have severe stress later in the semester.

Each member of the team must have at least 1 role on the project for which they will responsible. Likewise, every aspect of the project should have a team member assigned to it. Any task that does not clearly belong a team member is a potential source of controversy. You are free to decide these roles, though keep in mind that these roles will be used to make grading decisions. If one of your project submissions is unacceptable, an attempt will be made to identify which area of the project is lacking. If it is only broken because of one area, only team member responsible for that area will receive a grade reduction. If the responsible member(s) cannot easily be determined based on these roles, the entire group will receive the grade reduction. For this reason it is recommended that the roles correspond aspects visible in the final software, are mutually exclusive, and collectively exhaustive. It may be impossible to achieve all of these, but the roles should be defined as clearly as possible nonetheless.

Recommended: If your group is struggling to define these roles you can try the Model-View-Controller (MVC) design pattern. This provides 3 clear(ish) areas that can be assigned, or sub-divided.

Note that all team members might not have equal work to do for each submission, but you should try to balance the amount of work done by each member throughout the whole semester.

In addition to every aspect of the project having a group member assigned to complete it, each aspect must have an assigned reviewer in the group (not the assigned member) who will review any code submitted in that area. Every member of the group must be a reviewer for at least one part of the project. You are free to assign these reviewers however you'd like. The reviewer will perform the code reviews for any code submitted to this area and maintain the integrity of the project. If the reviewer approves broken or buggy code, they are responsible and may be held accountable in the form of a grade reduction.


Dispute resolution

This section will detail the team's dispute resolution solution. Team members might argue over frameworks, languages, who's responsible for a specific task, or even whether to use tabs or spaces. Anything that is not clearly defined in this document has the potential to become a dispute. You won't be able to predict what will cause a conflict later in the semester and add them all to the contract, but you must outline a protocol that you will follow if a dispute does occur. This can be something ridiculous, fun, serious, or whatever, but it must give a clear resolution. All members agree that this is the method that will be used and will be bound to the results. This will help the team move forward when a deadlock is reached and neither side is willing to move off their positions.

Some examples:

  • Bring in a mediator (please define who will be the mediator)
  • Compete in a game (winner gets to make the decision)
  • Rock-paper-scissors or flip a coin (if you want random 50/50 decisions)

Whatever your team decides, it is recommended that you have fun with this section. You will be enforcing this protocol at times of high stress when you will likely need some light-hearted fun.


Additional Details

The above are the minimum requirements for this document. Add any other details that you'd like in this document. If the document is vague and unclear, only the course structure will be used and any decisions will be made at the instructor's discretion.

With the wide variety of projects and teams the course structure has to be somewhat vague. This is your opportunity to add structure that is specific to you, your team, and your project. Within reason, this contract will be used to make grading decisions where the course leaves areas undefined. For example, the course gives -1 to all team members if a video submission is missing or doesn't meet the criteria for its submission, but if you state in this contract that a specific team member is responsible for video submissions then only that team member will receive a grade reduction. Use this to your advantage to agree on and clearly specify exactly what each group member should contribute to the project this semester (This should be sounding redundant by now).

Some examples of recommended additions:

  • Specify a deadline for code submissions. The vanilla course allows students to submit a pull request right at a deadline. This puts the code reviewer in a very tough spot where they either have to trust the author and accept the code without review (risking their own grade) or refuse the request and potentially enter a dispute. This can be avoided by requiring pull requests to be submitted with ample time for review.
  • Assign the video, documentation, and other miscellaneous tasks to specific team members. If each team member's role is very clearly defined it will limit disputes and stress later in the semester.
