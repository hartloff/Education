== Practical Objective

Objective: Deploy intuitive software with a professional landing page.

Verify: The landing page and software must be designed for a non-technical audience. A non-technical user can be given a link to the landing page with no additional information and successfully use your software for its intended purpose and report testing feedback. This implies that the landing page and software are intuitive to navigate and use. There cannot be issues with the beta testing and no unnecessary confusion among the beta testers.
Note: This objective may be assessed throughout beta testing and grading decisions may not begin until the end of the collaboration phase. There may be a large number of beta testers using your software throughout this phase. Feedback from beta testers may be used as evidence for grading decisions, but will not directly determine any grades.

== Beta Release
Technical Requirement: Deploy your software for beta testers.
Verify: A non-technical user must be able to access and use your software for its intended purpose and provide feedback to your team. These users will not follow your readme or install your project from the source code. You must provide a deployment appropriate for your project (ex. Downloadable executables, deployed on a web server, available in an app store, etc).
Technical Requirement: Create a release from the master branch of your Beta version on GitHub as "v0.3.0"
Verify: This release will appear on your project page.

In this phase you will prepare a release of your software for non-technical beta testers. For this release you cannot assume your users can follow the technical readme and will not install your project from the source code. This means your project must be deployed in a manner appropriate for your project. Examples of a deployment include:

  • Host your software as a web app
  • Host binaries or installers available for download for a major platform (ex. Windows, OSX, Ubuntu, android, iOS).
  • Submit your software to be freely available on a public platform (ex. Play store, Steam, Kongregate). Note that this is very risky since your submission must be available by the end of this phase so you must submit early and hope for quick approval.

If you are not sure what is considered a deployment for your specific project please contact the course staff immediately to avoid confusion near the deadline.

In addition, the beta version of your project must be merged into your master branch and be tagged as release "v0.3.0" on GitHub.

== Landing Page
Technical Requirement: Create a landing page for your project designed for non-technical users.
Verify: On your project page, your project name will become a link to this landing page. Course staff will verify that this page contains the required content.

Your beta release must be accompanied by a landing page for your project that includes instructions relevant to beta testers. This site is separate from anything on the course website and you cannot assume visitors to this site know anything about your project. This implies that you cannot assume the users have seen any of your videos, though you can add any videos to your landing page.

This is the site you should/will share with anyone interested in the project and it can be thought of as a poster advertising the project. You can include any additional information you'd like to this page, but for the beta release you are required to have at least the following:

  • A self-contained description of your project
  • Describe how a non-technical user can access your deployed software
  • The method for reporting bugs to the team (Bugs must be reported through GitHub issues on your project's repository). Your team can add any additional steps you'd like your beta testers to follow.

You should also add any additional information that is specific to your project such as specific bugs to look for, which features to test, special testing credentials, etc. The more information you provide to your testers and the more specific questions you ask them, the more likely you are to receive meaningful feedback.

Add the link to your project details on the course website and verify that it appears on your project page.

*If your project is a web app the landing page can be the deployed app itself as long as it meets all the criteria of the landing page.

== Testing

There are no new features added for your beta release so you have 2 weeks to test your code an ensure proper functionality (Alpha testing). Although your testing will not affect your grade directly, you will be held accountable for any major bugs or errors that appear in your released software. For your beta release there may be many minor bug reports (this is the purpose of beta testing), but you should not release your beta version with any major bugs or broken features. The bugs reported should be such that they would have been very difficult to find in development.

It is highly recommended that your team agrees on a robust testing methodology so you can release your software with confidence. In addition, robust testing will also make code reviews much simpler for the team.

== YouTube Video
Requirement: Upload a video demo of your Beta release to YouTube that is shorter than 2 minutes.
Verify: This video will appear on your project page. Course staff will verify that it meets the requirements.

Complete a video demo of your beta release and upload it to YouTube. This video must show a working demo of all features in your MVP as described in your team contract, but updated with any changes made since the previous release. As with the previous 2 videos, this video will be directed towards a non-technical audience and you should not show any code or use any technical language in this video. However, this video can be used as reference for your beta testers if you post it on your landing page so it can be helpful to explain anything that will be relevant specifically to them.

This video must be shorter than 2 minutes. The goal is to show the end users how the software is supposed to be used.


Submission

== On-Boarding Preparation
Technical Requirement: Develop on-boarding documentation in your GitHub wiki.
Verify: Course staff will verify that there is some documentation. The quality of this documentation will be assessed throughout the collaboration phase of the project.
Technical Requirement: Setup tasks for outside developers.
Verify: These tasks will be displayed on your project page and will be marked for outside help. Course staff will verify that each team member has created and tagged at least 1 issue for outside help. Use the "help wanted" tag.

During the next phase of the project you will be completing a task for another team in the course, and your class mates will be completing tasks for your team. To facilitate this you must prepare your project for the on-boarding process and make it possible, or simple, for a developer to visit your project and contribute code. At the end of the next phase you will be assessed based on how difficult or easy it was for someone to contribute to your project. As with many aspects of this course, submitting quality work in this section will save you time, and possibly your grade, later in the course.


Wiki

Use your GitHub wiki to outline your project and provide everything needed for a new developer to join the team. An outside developer who is unfamiliar with the project should be able to contribute code to your project after reading the wiki without having to contact the team (Though they can, and should, contact the team via your Ryver forum, having a face-to-face meeting with them might be too difficult to schedule so the wiki should leave them with only quick questions). The wiki should also include contribution instructions such as how to create a pull request, which branches to use, which tags to use in issues, who to assign as a code reviewer, etc.

Your code itself should also be easy to follow and well-organized. Simplicity is critical here. Meaningful naming, comments where needed, intuitive directory structure, etc. Try to imagine looking at your repository for the first time. Does it make sense? Is it confusing? Are there things that would need explaining? If so, adjust as needed.


Tasks

Prepare your outside tasks to be completed by students outside your group during the next phase of the project. These tasks do not have to be substantial, but they must have some impact on your final product (ie. adding comments is not acceptable). These tasks must be tagged with "help wanted" in GitHub issues.

Each team member must create at least 1 task for outside help that falls under their area of the project as defined by the team contract.

Recommended: Assigning outside tasks for an add-on feature can reduce your workload for the next submission
